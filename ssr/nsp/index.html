Notes on the Null Space Property

(Based on readings in the References)

1. Why does min∥x∥1 s.t. Ax=b result in sparse signals whereas min∥x∥2 s.t. Ax=b is less likely to yield sparse signals ?

The null space of A captures the set of all vectors that satisfy Ax=b, i.e. the feasible set. min∥x∥1 is desirable because of the geometry of how the ℓ1 ball intersects with the null space of A along sparse directions where one of the coordinates is 0. min∥x∥1 fails to intersect at a unique point only when the feasible set is the line aligns with slope (1,-1) in the example provided.

However the ℓ2 ball intersects with the null space of A such that the solution
are dense. In 2 dimensions for instance both (x,y) coordinates are non-zero.

Reasons to choose ℓ1. The closest ℓp norm that provides both sparse solutions and solves a convex problem. Additionally ℓ1 and ℓ0 provide equivalent solutions for sufficiently sparse signals.

2. According to this 1, why is the following true ?

A sensing matrix Am×n has the null space property of order s if and only if, for all ∥x0∥≤s, x0 is the unique minimizer of

min∥x∥1 s.t. Ax=Ax0

  • If the NSP fails, then exact recovery fails:
    - Let v be any element from the null space of A, v∈(A)∖{0}.
    - Suppose the null space of the sensing matrix A contain at least one sparse signal over the support S. Then the cardinality of v over the non-sparse support Sc is small i.e. ∥vSc∥1≤∥vS∥1. Thus, the null space property is violated since a non-zero sparse vector cannot satisfy ∥vS∥1<∥vSc∥1.
    - Any vector v=vS in the null space satisfies Ax=A(x+v)=b and thus belongs in the feasible set. For any minimizer x0, one can construct another signal x0+(−vS) that also solves the problem min∥x∥1 s.t. Ax=Ax0.
    - Or in other words if ∥x0∥1<s then

    ∥x0+v∥1=∥xS,0+vS+vSc∥1=∥xS,0+vS∥1+∥vSc∥1≤∥x0∥1

    - Thus we no longer have a unique minimizer x0

  • If ERP succeeds then NSP is satisfied:

Illustrate the null space property.

Example in 2-dimensions: If the sparse support is on y-axis. Then the set of all vectors such that x-coefficients are large will satisfy the null space property. (This forms a cone i.e. if any vector is in the set, then all scalar multiplicatives are also in the set). The cone should be away from the descent directions away from the ℓ1 ball.

Example in 3-dimensions: Let x0=(0,0,c). Then we want to consider vectors that satisfy |v3|<|v1|+|v2|. If the sparse support is a vector, then the null space of A should contain sufficiently dense 3-dimensional vectors. Here we would consider the orthants that point away from the octohedron that captures the ℓ1 ball in 3-dim
